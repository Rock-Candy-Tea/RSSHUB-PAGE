"Just sad, really sad and really angry. I didn't have the words to express how I was feeling," she said.</p>  <p>Zhang said she's avoided going out during the pandemic out of fear of being targeted by racist behaviour.</p>  <p>Thy-Anh Nguyen and her cousin, Chi Vu, came to the march to show their support for Asian women and the local communities — but also for their elders, who they said are especially vulnerable to discrimination.</p>  <p>"With all the hate crime going on right now, I'm always thinking about my parents," Nguyen said.</p>  <p>Wong, the first person of Chinese descent elected to the city council, said Sunday's rally was also a "great moment of solidarity" for local communities.</p>  <p>"It is one of the first times the Chinese and Asian community has come together in Montreal, protesting and talking about racism," she said. "And I think this is a positive step toward recognizing this issue in Montreal."</p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia image full"><div class="placeholder"><div their elders.<!-- --> <!-- -->(Simon Nakonechny/CBC)</figcaption></figure></span></div>  <h2>'Hurdles to overcome' in addressing anti-Asian racism</h2>  <p>Montrealer Anne Beaulieu, one of the organizers of the march, told <a href="https://www.cbc.ca/listen/live-radio/1-15-daybreak-montreal">CBC Montreal's <em>Daybreak</em></a> she felt she needed to take action to denounce the Atlanta attacks. </p>  <p>Her Facebook group, Groupe D'entraide contre le racisme envers les asiatiques au Québec, organized the march along with Progressive Chinese of Quebec and sex workers advocacy group Stella to honour the victims of the shooting and to denounce ongoing acts of racism against Asian communities.  </p>  <p>May Chiu, a member of Progressive Chinese of Quebec, said she wanted the march to provide a safe space for people to call out issues of racism. </p>  <p>"I think that there's a challenge in talking about racism, understanding racism, the different forms of racism," Chiu said. "And when you talk about specifically anti-Asian racism, again we have another few hurdles to overcome. People don't know how to identify it." </p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/toronto/asian-racism-canada-atlanta-shootings-1.5953800" class="similarLink" data-contentid flag text="'Stand with us': Anti-Asian racism in Canada is nothing new, communities say after Atlanta shootings"><span class="similarLinkText">'Stand with us': Anti-Asian racism in Canada is nothing new, communities say after Atlanta shootings</span></a></li></ul></span></div>  <p>Chiu said many people still think racism can only be a physical act, but said she and many others have to deal with racist verbal attacks and microaggressions on a daily basis.</p>  <p>Chiu, a Montreal lawyer who strives to defend the human rights of others, said she sometimes feels like her own rights are being trampled on. </p>  <p>"I go to court almost every week and almost every week I'm questioned as to whether I am a lawyer, if I belong in the courthouse," Chiu told <em>Daybreak</em>. </p>  <p>Beaulieu said she's also faced racist incidents, but things have gotten even worse since the pandemic started.  She suggested that the Montreal police statistics may not fully encompass the actual number of crimes that took place.</p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/world/suspect-atlanta-massage-parlour-shootings-1.5953125" class="similarLink"
