many have questions about what the experience is like.</p> </div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> Here, New York Times science and health reporters answer some frequently asked questions about vaccination.</p> </div></div>  <div data-type="header" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <h2 class="h5 heavy-text"> Will it hurt?</h2></div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> The jab feels like any other vaccination. Sore arms after the shot are common. Many who have received the vaccine likened the arm pain to that of a flu shot; for others, it was considerably worse. <i>—Amy Harmon</i></p> </div></div> <div data-type="header" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <h2 class="h5 heavy-text"> Why do I have to wait after I get the shot?</h2></div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> This allows health workers to monitor you for any signs of an allergic reaction, which are rare.</p> </div></div>   <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> Make sure you bring a good three-layer mask or plan to double mask at your vaccination appointment. Some have reported being sent to crowded waiting rooms with other recently vaccinated individuals. If you encounter a crowded waiting room, move to a hallway or somewhere with fewer people. Everyone should stay masked and keep their distance. <i>—Tara Parker-Pope</i></p> </div></div> <div data-type="header" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <h2 class="h5 heavy-text"> How long might it take for side effects to show up?</h2></div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> Side effects might show up within one to three days after vaccination and could last for one to three days after symptoms start. Sore arms, muscle pain, fatigue, headaches and fevers are common. Some people experience only mild discomfort, while others report severe fatigue, aches and other flu-like symptoms.</p> </div></div>   <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class data-item-type="depthscroll" data-item-id="depth_scroll_middle" data-item-number="middle"> Call a doctor if your side effects are causing you worry, or they don’t seem to be going away after a few days. <i>—Dani Blum</i></p> </div></div> <div data-type="image" class="crd card-captioned clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <figure class="cls-height" data-trk-photo-credit="Craig <style>img.full-width1&#123;flex-shrink:0;object-fit:contain;min-height:50%;height:auto;width:100%&#125;</style></div></div> <div data-type="header" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <h2 class="h5 heavy-text"> Can I take an over-the-counter pain reliever to treat side effects?</h2></div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> Yes, but timing matters. You shouldn’t try to stave off discomfort by taking a pain reliever <i>before</i> getting the shot. Pretreatment could blunt your response to the vaccine.</p> </div></div>  <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> Experts agree it’s safe to take a pain reliever or fever reducer such as ibuprofen or acetaminophen to relieve discomfort <i>after</i> getting vaccinated. Don’t take it right away — you may not need it. But if you develop aches or a headache after the shot, it’s safe to use a nonprescription pain reliever. <i>—Tara Parker-Pope</i></p> </div></div> <div data-type="header" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <h2 class="h5 heavy-text"> If I’ve been vaccinated, why do I still need to wear a mask?</h2></div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> It takes awhile for the vaccine’s protection to kick in. You aren’t fully vaccinated until about two weeks after your final dose.</p> </div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> After full vaccination, you’ll still need to wear a mask in public spaces. Millions more people remain unprotected, and there’s no way to easily discern who has been vaccinated and who hasn’t. And while the vaccine will protect you from serious illness, it’s still possible for a vaccinated person to contract the virus and spread it to others. <i>—Tara Parker-Pope</i></p> </div></div> <div data-type="header" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <h2 class="h5 heavy-text"> What can I do after I’m vaccinated?</h2></div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class="stop-here"> The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention has good news for people struggling with pandemic isolation: Fully vaccinated Americans can safely gather indoors with other vaccinated people — no mask required. Imagine mask-free dinner parties with vaccinated friends!</p> </div></div>  <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class> Vaccinated people also can visit indoors with unvaccinated people from one other household, as long as the unvaccinated people are at low risk. This means vaccinated grandparents can once again visit grandchildren, even if the children aren’t yet vaccinated. <i>—Roni Caryn Rabin </i></p> </div></div> <div data-type="text" class="crd clln--it"> <div class=" crd--cnt "> <p class data-item-type="depthscroll" data-item-id="depth_scroll_bottom" data-item-number="bottom"> <i>c.2021 The New York Times Company</i></p> <div class="spaced spaced-md spaced-bottom hddn-mble"></div> <div class="spaced spaced-sm spaced-bottom hddn-dktp hddn-tbl"></div> </div></div> </div>  </div> <div id="f0b6YJJV5Q0Xps" class="pb-async pb-feature"></div> <div id="fQNMYA1V5Q0Xps" class="pb-async pb-feature"></div>  <div class="pb-container"> </div>
