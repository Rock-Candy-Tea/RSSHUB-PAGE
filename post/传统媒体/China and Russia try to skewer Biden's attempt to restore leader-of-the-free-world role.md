Brown/Reuters)</figcaption></figure><div class="story"><span><p>Joe Biden promised to restore a more familiar mentality in American foreign policy after his predecessor spent four years <a href="https://abcnews.go.com/Politics/compiling-donald-trumps-recent-comments-allies-rivals/story?id=56618619"><u>deriding</u></a> the U.S.'s traditional democratic allies as trade cheats and deadbeats.</p>  <p>Biden's proposed approach involved renewing a leader-of-the-free-world ethos from the last century to<a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/world/biden-foreign-policy-canada-1.5883065"><u> rally democratic</u></a> friends against this century's authoritarians.</p><section id="inread-wrapper-id-102889258"></section>  <p>This week, the authoritarians hit back.</p>  <p>On successive days, the U.S.'s two biggest geopolitical rivals, China and Russia, lashed out at Biden and the country he leads.</p>  <p>Some analysts view it as an effort from those countries to establish terms for the relationship during Biden's four-year mandate.</p>  <p>"When you are dealing with a new partner, you want to test them out, first and foremost," said Lynette Ong, a China expert at the University of Toronto.</p>  <p>"Secondly, you want to show your strength."</p>  <p>The Chinese government cast its broadside in far wider terms than simply disparaging Biden — it belittled America's democratic self-identity.</p>  <p>It laid bare new challenges in being the unofficial leader of the free world. </p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia image full"><div class="placeholder"><div class="placeholderImage" style="padding-bottom:56.77%"></div><img loading="lazy" alt Putin <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-russia-usa-reaction-idUSKBN2BA0S1"><u>replied</u></a> with a put-down reminiscent of a playground taunt: "He who said it, did it."</p>  <p>Then a memorable scene unfolded in Alaska where Biden administration officials met with their Chinese counterparts for the first time.</p>  <p>The world's older and newer superpowers engaged in an unusually trenchant airing of grievances in front of TV cameras.</p>  <h2>A memorable moment for two superpowers</h2>  <p>Depending on who you ask, the other guy started it. </p>  <p>In the U.S. view, all it did was allude — in passing — to Beijing's enslavement of Uyghurs; bullying of smaller countries, including Canada; its hacking and intellectual property theft; and squashing of Hong Kong's freedoms.</p>  <p>The stage for a tense meeting had been set in the preceding days. </p>  <p>The U.S. slapped sanctions on <a href="https://apnews.com/article/beijing-hong-kong-wang-chen-china-national-security-b5b1d92f37ca1fa6ac46ce40ccb7b123"><u>24 Chinese officials</u></a> over clampdowns on freedom in Hong Kong, <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-china-commerce/u-s-subpoenas-chinese-communications-firms-in-probe-of-national-security-risks-idUKKBN2B92OH"><u>subpoenaed</u></a> multiple Chinese high-tech companies to see if they pose intelligence risks, and <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-china-telecom/fcc-begins-effort-to-revoke-two-chinese-telecoms-u-s-operating-authority-idUSKBN2B92FE?il=0"><u>revoked licences</u></a> from two Chinese telecom firms.</p>  <p>China had restricted the use of <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-tesla-china/chinese-military-bans-tesla-cars-in-its-complexes-on-camera-concerns-sources-idUSKBN2BB18R"><u>Tesla</u></a> vehicles and held a trial Friday for Canadian <a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/world/spavor-kovrig-michaels-trial-spying-1.5955938"><u>Michael Spavor</u></a>, barring diplomats from attending.</p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia "[Our] relationship with China," he said, "will be competitive where it should be, collaborative where it can be, adversarial where it must be."</p>  <p>That apparently set off his Chinese interlocutors.</p>  <p>Veteran foreign affairs official Yang Jiechi proceeded with a lengthy denunciation of the United States — and insisted that journalists remain in the room to hear him unload while American press handlers tried in vain to escort them out.</p>  <p>Yang spoke of foreign wars started by the U.S. He also accused the U.S. of abusing its financial might to bend other countries to its will; of mistreating Black Americans; of being a hypocrite when it accuses other countries of cyberattacks; and of interfering in Chinese internal affairs in Xinjiang, Tibet and Taiwan.</p> uppercut aimed at a particularly sensitive spot, Yang questioned the state of American democracy.</p>  <p>"The United States [should] change its own image and stop advancing its own democracy in the rest of the world," he said. "Many people within the United States actually have little confidence in the democracy of the United States."</p>  <p>He also said China hopes the U.S. "will do better on human rights."</p>  <p>Blinken interjected with one more defence of his country. </p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia image full"><div class="placeholder"><div class="placeholderImage" Brown/Reuters)</figcaption></figure></span></div>  <p>As imperfect as the United States is, he said, one thing it does right is to allow open and free discussion about its own flaws in an attempt to be better.</p>  <h2>The uncomfortable point about U.S. democracy</h2>  <p>But the point raised by Yang about democracy cuts to uncomfortable truths.</p>  <p>If the United States is to be an apostle of democracy, it's a less committed messenger preaching to a more skeptical global audience. </p>  <p>Other countries' <a href="https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2021/01/19/even-before-capitol-riot-most-people-in-germany-france-and-the-uk-had-concerns-about-u-s-political-system/"><u>views of the state of American democracy</u></a> have plunged.</p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/world/russia-biden-trump-election-interference-1.5952076" class="similarLink" data-contentid flag text="Russia interfered in U.S. election, likely at Putin's behest, intelligence report says"><span class="similarLinkText">Russia interfered in U.S. election, likely at Putin's behest, intelligence report says</span></a></li></ul></span></div>  <p>Americans themselves have not only been losing trust <u><a href="https://news.gallup.com/poll/1597/confidence-institutions.aspx">in their own institutions</a>,</u> they may have minimal interest in promoting them abroad.</p>  <p>Last month a Pew Research poll of 2,596 U.S. respondents put promoting democracy <a href="https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2021/03/02/americans-put-low-priority-on-promoting-democracy-abroad-2/"><u>as dead last</u></a> among And Biden has re-entered global institutions such as the World Health Organization and Paris climate accord, and promised a more collaborative approach at the World Trade Organization, where tensions with China threatened to pull it apart.</p>  <h2>Co-operation still possible</h2>  <p>One senior Asia official in the former Obama administration warns against reading too much into the events of this week.</p>  <p>Daniel Russel, the former <a href="https://2009-2017.state.gov/r/pa/ei/biog/212045.htm"><u>assistant secretary of state</u></a> and Asian affairs aide to Barack Obama, noted that Biden hasn't yet completed key <a href="https://www.defense.gov/Explore/News/Article/Article/2495328/global-posture-review-will-tie-strategy-defense-policy-to-basing/#:~:text=The%20global%20posture%20review%20will,national%20interests%2C%22%20Kirby%20said."><u>foreign policy reviews</u></a>.</p>  <p>"It was an unfortunate beginning," he said of the Alaska meeting.</p>  <p>"But I wouldn't overinterpret it either. It certainly doesn't mean that the United States and China can't communicate or are condemned to perpetual strategic rivalry."</p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/politics/china-responsible-genocide-independent-report-1.5942289" class="similarLink" data-contentid flag text="Chinese government responsible for genocide in Xinjiang, says independent report"><span class="similarLinkText">Chinese government responsible for genocide in Xinjiang, says independent report</span></a></li></ul></span></div>  <p>Ian Johnson, a Pulitzer Prize-winning former China correspondent, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2021/03/19/opinion/us-china-biden.html"><u>wrote</u></a> in the New York Times that there are easy ways for the U.S. to build goodwill, including by restoring suspended educational, diplomatic and journalistic visas.</p>  <p>But he said he fears the decay in U.S.-China relations is becoming dangerous.</p>  <p>Next week, the Chinese and Russian foreign ministers <a href="https://www.scmp.com/news/china/diplomacy/article/3126210/china-russia-stand-back-back-tensions-us-rise-observers-say"><u>meet</u></a>, with plans to <a href="https://www.globaltimes.cn/page/202103/1218769.shtml"><u>bolster their own alliance</u></a>, intended to counter what might, in another era, have been referred to as the free world, and its leader, Joe Biden.</p></span></div>
