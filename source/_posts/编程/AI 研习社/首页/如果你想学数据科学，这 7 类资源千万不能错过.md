it is ‘science’. Data science is only useful when the data are used to answer a question. That is the science part of the equation. The problem with this view of data science is that it is much harder than the view that focuses on data size or tools. It is much, much easier to calculate the size of a data set and say ‘My data are bigger than yours’ or to say, ‘I can code in Hadoop, can you?’ than to say, ‘I have this really hard question, can I answer it with my data?’.”</p><p>Data Science is old. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Graunt" target="_blank">John Graunt</a> did it when it was cool. Literally cool. It was during the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Little-Ice-Age" target="_blank">Little Ice Age</a> in the XV century that he developed one of the first works in demography using probabilistic models. By 1960 the subject was already very mature and called <a href="https://dl.acm.org/citation.cfm?doid=365719.366510" target="_blank"><em>datalogy</em></a><em>, </em>but it was only in 2012 when Harvard Business Review published the article “<a href="https://hbr.org/2012/10/data-scientist-the-sexiest-job-of-the-21st-century" target="_blank">Data Scientist: the Sexiest Job of the 21st Century</a>” that the term — and the job — increased in popularity. A great offer of online courses on the subject would be available only two years after this. Google Trends shows something interesting: although the term “Statistician” was trending downwards since 2004, the term “Data Scientist” had a stronger positive movement after HBR’s article and it was boosted when the offer of online courses increased.</p><p><br></p><p><img src="https://static.leiphone.com/uploads/new/sns/article/201811/1541658212374394.png" referrerpolicy="no-referrer"></p><p><br></p><p><a href="https://trends.google.com/trends/explore?date=all&q=Data%20Scientist,%2Fm%2F0c_xl" target="_blank">https://trends.google.com/trends/explore?date=all&q=Data%20Scientist,%2Fm%2F0c_xl</a></p><p>With that in mind, I need to make it clear: data scientists are not statisticians and they don’t replace statisticians (and vice-versa), but a genuine interest in statistics and maths is key for achieving proper data science. I am not a statistician myself, but the very first resource below made me love the theme.</p><p>Please also observe how the resources below focus more on “curiosity” and “understanding” rather than “applying”.</p><p><br></p><p><img src="https://static.leiphone.com/uploads/new/sns/article/201811/1541658212288042.png" referrerpolicy="no-referrer"></p><p><br></p><p>Img source: <a href="https://towardsdatascience.com/introduction-to-statistics-e9d72d818745" target="_blank">https://towardsdatascience.com/introduction-to-statistics-e9d72d818745</a></p><h3>Do you like lists?</h3><p>In a nutshell the 7 resources are:</p><ol class=" list-paddingleft-2"><li><p>The Drunkard’s Walk, book by Leonard Mlodinow</p></li><li><p>Machine Learning Course, Created by Stanford University and taught by Dr. <a href="https://medium.com/@andrewng" target="_blank">Andrew Ng</a></p></li><li><p>Introduction to Mathematical Thinking, also by Stanford and taught by Dr. <a href="https://medium.com/@profkeithdevlin" target="_blank">Keith Devlin</a></p></li><li><p>Coding</p></li><li><p>A prejudice-free review on Maths</p></li><li><p>People</p></li><li><p>Reading about Heterodox and Orthodox Economics</p></li></ol><p>The list is neither in chronological nor climactic order. years ago (2009?) I wasn’t really impressed when I’ve got this book as a Secret Santa present, but it turned out to be one of my all time favourites.</p><p><a href="https://www.amazon.co.uk/Drunkards-Walk-Randomness-Rules-Lives/dp/0141026472/ref=sr_1_1?s=books&ie=UTF8&qid=1537385919&sr=1-1&keywords=The+drunkards+walk" target="_blank"><em>The Drunkard’s Walk</em></a> is about understanding randomness’ influence in our lives and “reveals the psychological illusions that prevent us understanding everything from stock-picking to wine-tasting”, according to Amazon’s product description.</p><p>It has enlightened me to realise how easily one can become a victim of <em>chance </em>and to understand why explaining all factors leading to an occurrence is much simpler than predicting when — or if — similar occurrences will ever happen again.</p><p>From the analytics perspective, this book shows the importance of predictive models and demonstrates the history behind statistical analysis, also showing how data can be used to answer hard questions and how some of these questions will remain unanswered. I recall this as my first contact with “data science”.</p><p>Another good read in the same style: <a href="http://algorithms%20to%20live%20by:%20The%20Computer%20Science%20of%20Human%20Decisions/" target="_blank">Algorithms to Live by: The Computer Science of Human Decisions</a>.</p><p><br></p><p><img src="https://static.leiphone.com/uploads/new/sns/article/201811/1541658213953309.png" referrerpolicy="no-referrer"></p><p><br></p><p>Representation of a random walk. It was mine. A must have, in my opinion.</p><p>It first gives you a revision on key concepts of linear algebra and teaches you basic Matlab (or Octave) programming. Only then the very first concepts of Regression with one variable will be presented. Further on the course will walk you through intriguing parts such as Principal Component Analysis and Neural Networks, but if you are serious about learning it with proper reviews, taking quizzes and grasping concepts beyond the course, this will definitely foster your intuition and teach you key artefacts and the maths used in machine learning.</p><p><br></p><p><img src="https://static.leiphone.com/uploads/new/sns/article/201811/1541658214445766.png" referrerpolicy="no-referrer"></p><p><br></p><p>Example slide from the course. Source: <a href="https://tinyurl.com/ybupqzzb" target="_blank">https://tinyurl.com/ybupqzzb</a></p><p>Introduction to Mathematical Thinking</p><p>I took discrete maths at uni back in 2008 and I loved it! Even still, I decided to review core concepts and boost skills using this course provided by Stanford University via <a href="https://www.coursera.org/learn/mathematical-thinking" target="_blank">Coursera</a>. The course covers part of what I saw in the first term back then and it bolstered my analytical thinking capabilities. It can easily become a new paradigm to a lot of people as the mathematical thinking proposed in the course differs a lot from the ordinary reasoning process, especially that of common written language. Besides, the last few sessions can become rather challenging on the mathematical-proofing-side-of-things. Unavoidably it will make you feel like learning a new spoken language.</p><p>You won’t mind learning this “new language” because critical thinking is crucial for data science. An important add-on (or painless alternative) to this course is grasping concepts of common fallacies, to avoid — or detect — them during exploratory data analysis or getting insights from data for example.</p><p><a href="http://utminers.utep.edu/omwilliamson/ENGL1311/fallacies.htm" title="http://utminers.utep.edu/omwilliamson/ENGL1311/fallacies.htm">Master List of Logical Fallacies<br>utminers.utep.edu/omwilliamson/emgl1311utminers.utep.edu</a><a href="http://utminers.utep.edu/omwilliamson/ENGL1311/fallacies.htm"></a></p><p>Lock yourself in a cage for the first few weeks after starting these courses as the excitement can make you over criticise a lot of things you hear and read. Remember the goal: data science!</p><p><br></p><p><img src="https://static.leiphone.com/uploads/new/sns/article/201811/1541658212729848.png" referrerpolicy="no-referrer"></p><p><br></p><p>Dilbert listing some common components in fallacies. <p>来让我看看细节部分吧！</p><p>醉汉漫步（译者注：这是一本书，豆瓣地址：https://book.douban.com/subject/3102009/）</p><p>许多年前（大概是2009年？）当我在圣诞节收到这本书时，对它并没有很深的印象，但是多年后它变成了我一直以来的最爱。</p><p>醉汉漫步这本书是帮助我们理解随机性对我们生活造成的影响，亚马逊的产品描述是这样的“它揭露了这些心理错觉是如何阻挡我们理解生活，从选股到品酒”。</p><p>它帮助我觉察到我们是多么容易成为概率的受害者，也帮助我理解为什么我们更容易推到理解导致事件发生的所有因素，而预测类似情况会不会再次发生却很难的原因。</p><p>从分析者的角度来说，这本书向我们展示了预测模型的重要性也从统计数据的角度证明了历史事件发生的必然性，同样还向我们展示了如何使用数据来解答一些难以理解的问题，以及为什么有些问题仍旧无法获得答案。我把这本书认为是我同“数据科学”的第一次亲密接触。</p><p>另外一本很好的同类读物是：《<a href="https://tinyurl.com/ybupqzzb" target="_blank" style="font-size:
