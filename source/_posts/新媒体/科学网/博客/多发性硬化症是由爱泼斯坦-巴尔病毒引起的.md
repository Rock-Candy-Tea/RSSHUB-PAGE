Using data from millions of US military recruits monitored over a 20-year period, Bjornevik <em>et al</em>. determined that Epstein-Barr virus infection greatly increased the risk of subsequent multiple sclerosis and that it preceded the development of disease, supporting its potential role in the pathogenesis of multiple sclerosis (see the Perspective by Robinson and Steinman). —YN</span></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><a href="https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.abj8222" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "arial black", "avant garde";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "arial black", "avant garde";">Abstract</span></a></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic inflammatory demyelinating disease of the central nervous system of unknown etiology.
