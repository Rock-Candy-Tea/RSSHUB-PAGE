So, impending deficiency for both life and technology is always looming. For humans and other animals, cobalt is the active center of vitamin B<sub>12</sub> involved in methyl, and other, biochemistries. Although it is toxic in excess, we need <span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "MS Gothic";">～</span>40 ng of Co daily, while other animals need their proportionate share, as do many microbes. In vitamin B<sub>12</sub>, the variable valence metal is coordinated within a corrin ring by four nitrogen ligands. The same Co-bearing core, although attached to iron sulfide, is central also to life’s acetyl coenzyme-A pathway (</span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-1" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">1</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">). Indeed, Co had, and has, a vital part to play in the emergence and evolution of both the animate and the technological systems. Right down at the bottom of our evolutionary tree, the acetogens and the methanogens require such Co corrinoid iron sulfide enzymes for methyl biochemistry (</span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-2" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">2</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">). In industry, cobalt is best known today as an obligatory constituent of batteries and smartphones as well as wear-resistant tools, whereas it was already playing a central role in the chemical industry as the “cobalt blue” of paints and pigments in the 19th century and as a catalyst in the 20th century.</span></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">In PNAS, He et al. (</span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-3" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">3</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">) revisit cobalt’s catalytic propensities as they explore the hydrothermal synthesis of long-chain hydrocarbons from sodium bicarbonate as substrate. Previous geochemical experiments on hydrothermal syntheses of hydrocarbons involved the hydrogenation of CO as in the Fischer–Tropsch reaction—a reaction assumed to operate during exothermic serpentinization reactions produced by waters convecting within ultramafic (Mg > Fe silicate) rocks (</span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-1" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">1</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">, </span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-3" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">3</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">, </span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-4" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">4</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">). Past work on the geochemical reduction of CO<sub>2</sub> to long-chained hydrocarbons using F–T syntheses apparently generated polymers ranging from C<sub>2</sub> to >C<sub>35</sub> from aqueous solutions of formic acid in stainless steel vessels at 175 <span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: 宋体;">℃</span> (</span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-4" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">4</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">). However, these previous attempts to synthesize the abiotic generation of methane and the higher hydrocarbons have been called into question due to a variety of possible contaminants, from steel autoclaves, to the use of rubber bungs, to the use of methane-sorbed rocks employed in various attempts at reducing CO<sub>2</sub> entrained in serpentinizing solutions (</span><a href="https://www.pnas.org/content/119/3/e2121307119#ref-5" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">5</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">).</span></p><p><br></p>                    <br><br>
