font-family: "times new roman";"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: 宋体;">他们的研究发现，当动物清醒时，鸽子大脑消耗的葡萄糖量惊人地低（每</span>100<span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: 宋体;">克每分钟</span>27.29<span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">卡娅·冯·尤根认为，这些差异可能与鸟类的体温升高或大脑的特定布局有关。鸟类的大脑平均也比哺乳动物的大脑小。但它们的大脑仍保持着令人印象深刻的能力，部分原因可能是它们的神经元数量较多，但成本更低。</span></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">卡娅·冯·尤根说：“我们的发现解释了鸟类如何能够在不影响处理能力的情况下支持如此多的神经元。在鸟类和哺乳动物漫长的平行进化过程中，鸟类进化出了更小的大脑，大脑中有大量的神经元，这些神经元具有高级认知能力。而且，似乎鸟类特有的元素——神经元大小很小、体温高和特定的鸟脑布局——的综合作用，可能在以更高的效率处理信息方面产生了一个可能的优势：具有高级处理能力的廉价神经元。”</span></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">研究人员表示，他们现在想更多地了解鸟类神经元如何消耗更少的葡萄糖。虽然他们对这可能的工作方式有想法，但还需要进一步的研究和测试来揭示“鸟类如何获得如此高的神经元处理效率的确切机制解释。”</span></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft</span></a><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";"> through Gu 227/16-1 and Gu 227/21-1<span style="font-size: 18px; font-size: 18px; font-family: "arial black", "avant garde";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "arial black", "avant garde";">Summary</span></a></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">Brains are among the most energetically costly tissues in the mammalian body.<sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2#bib1" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 18px; color: #005789;">1</span></a></sup> This is predominantly caused by expensive neurons with high glucose demands.<sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2#bib2" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 18px; color: #005789;">2</span></a></sup> Across mammals, the neuronal energy budget appears to be fixed, possibly posing an evolutionary constraint on brain growth.<sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 18px; color: #005789;">3</span></a>, </sup> <sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 18px; color: #005789;">4</span></a>, </sup> <sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 18px; color: #005789;">5</span></a>, </sup> <sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; We estimated glucose metabolism using positron emission tomography (PET) and 2-[<sup>18</sup>F]fluoro-2-deoxyglucose ([<sup>18</sup>F]FDG) as the radiotracer in awake and anesthetized pigeons. Combined with kinetic modeling, this is the gold standard to quantify cerebral metabolic rate of glucose consumption (CMR<sub>glc</sub>).<sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2#bib8" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 18px; color: #005789;">8</span></a></sup> We found that neural tissue in the pigeon consumes 27.29 ± 1.57 μmol glucose per 100 g per min in an awake state, which translates into a surprisingly low neuronal energy budget of 1.86 × 10<sup>−9</sup> ± 0.2 × 10<sup>−9</sup> μmol glucose per neuron per minute. This is approximately 3 times lower than the rate in the average mammalian neuron.<sup><a href="https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822(22)01219-2#bib3" target="_blank"><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 18px; color: #005789;">3</span></a></sup> The remarkably low neuronal energy budget explains how pigeons, and possibly other avian species, can support such high numbers of neurons without associated metabolic costs or compromising neuronal signaling. The advantage in neuronal processing of information at a higher efficiency possibly emerged during the distinct evolution of the avian brain.</span></p>                    <br><br>
