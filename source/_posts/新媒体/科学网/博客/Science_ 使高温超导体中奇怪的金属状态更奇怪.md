font-family: "times new roman";">Cuprate superconductors host a number of complex phases, and elucidating their relationship to each other may shed light on the nature of cuprate superconductivity. Two of these intriguing phases are the strange metal phase and the charge-density wave (CDW) order, which appear next to each other in the cuprate phase diagram. The hallmark of the strange metal phase, the linear dependence of electrical resistivity on temperature, disappears in the CDW phase. Wahlberg <em>et al</em>. strained thin films of the cuprate YBa<sub><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 7px;">2</span></sub>Cu<sub><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 7px;">3</span></sub>O<sub><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 7px;">7–δ</span></sub> to suppress CDW order (see the Perspective by Le Tacon). Using resonant inelastic x-ray scattering and transport measurements, the researchers found that suppressing CDW restored the linear-in-temperature resistivity in the CDW portion of the phase diagram. —JS</span></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abc8372" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 18px; font-family: "arial black", "avant garde";" target="_blank"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "arial black", "avant garde";">Abstract</span></a></p><p style="line-height: 2em;"><span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: "times new roman";">The normal state of optimally doped cuprates is dominated by the “strange metal” phase that shows a linear temperature (<em>T</em>) dependence of the resistivity persisting down to the lowest <em>T.</em> For underdoped cuprates, this behavior is lost below the pseudogap temperature <em>T</em>*, where charge density waves (CDWs), together with other intertwined local orders, characterize the ground state. We found that the <em>T</em>-linear resistivity of highly strained, ultrathin, underdoped YBa<sub><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 7px;">2</span></sub>Cu<sub><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 7px;">3</span></sub>O<sub><span style="font-family: "times new roman"; font-size: 7px;">7–δ</span></sub> films is restored when the CDW amplitude, detected by resonant inelastic x-ray scattering, is suppressed. This observation suggests an intimate connection between the onset of CDWs and the departure from <em>T</em>-linear resistivity in underdoped cuprates. Our results illustrate the potential of using strain control to manipulate the ground state of quantum materials.</span></p>                    <br><br>
